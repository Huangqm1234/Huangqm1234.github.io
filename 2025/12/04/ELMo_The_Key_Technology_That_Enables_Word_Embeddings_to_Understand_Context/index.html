ELMo: The Key Technology That Enables Word Embeddings to Understand Context
Qianming Huang Three
  2025-12-05 11:39:09   2025-12-05 23:50:44  

Why Did ELMo Become a Milestone in NLP?

In the early days of NLP, Word2Vec and GloVe were true game-changers.

Word2Vec (2013) transformed words into low-dimensional vectors, allowing computers to compute semantic distances between words. For example, King−Man+Woman=Queen.

The, GloVe (2014) refined this idea and produced even more semantically accurate embeddings.

However, they both shared a fundamental limitation: one word always corresponds to one Fixed vector, regardless of context. In other words, they produced static word embeddings.

In natural language, polysemy is really common. “Apple” can mean the fruit or the tech company. “Play” can mean to play sports in “play soccer,” but a theater or a drama in a Broadway play.

Static word embeddings can not distinguish any of these. They assign one single average vector to a word, such as “Apple”, blending its different meanings into a single representation vector. It’s like trying to use one photograph to represent a person in every possible situation. Those static word embeddings simply can not adapt to different contexts.

At this point, context awareness became a critical need. Embedding from Language Models (ELMo) solves this problem by introducing Deep + Contextualized Word Embeddings. Its core idea is really simple. It uses a bidirectional Language Model (biLM) that reads the entire sentence, from left-to-right and right-to-left, to capture contextual meaning.

So when interpreting the word “play” the model uses context (from left-to-right and right-to-left) to determine whether it means to play or a theatrical play.

Except contextualized word embeddings, deep is also the key of the ELMo. It combines information from all layers of the biLM:

  • Lower layers capture syntactic patterns.

  • Higher layers capture semantic meaning.

And it dynamically reweights these layers depending on the downstream task focusing more on syntax for POS tagging, and more on semantics for word-sense disambiguation.

ℹ️ Note

To help you understand, POS tagging is Part-of-Speech Tagging, which is tagging the part of speech for each word in the sentence, such as noun, verb, etc.

Thus, it not only solves the problem of polysemy, but also transforms word embeddings from static to dynamic, making it a true milestone in NLP.

What Exactly Is ELMo?

Earlier, we mentioned that the core of ELMo is deep + contextualized word embeddings.This means ELMo extracts word representations from a language model, allowing each embedding to change with the surrounding context. Every word vector is computed based on the entire sentence, and ELMo can flexibly draw on information from multiple layers of the language model.

For example, let us consider the word “apple” again. In the sentence “The apple is ripe and ready to eat”, ELMo focuses on context words like “ripe” and “eat,” producing a vector that leans toward the meaning of the fruit. But in “Apple just released a new phone”, ELMo instead attends to context like “new phone”, generating a vector that reflects the meaning of the tech company.

What’s more, ELMo selects information intelligently. Its underlying biLM consists of multiple layers, and ELMo can flexibly combine these layers based on the task at hand. In other words, it draws what it needs from different levels of the model, ensuring that each word embedding is used where it matters most.

In summary, the table below better illustrates the characteristics of ELMo and its comparison with static word embeddings.

Dimension Static Embeddings (Word2Vec / GloVe) ELMo Dynamic Embeddings
Generation Basis Global statistics of individual words Full sentence-level context
Number of Vectors One word → One fixed vector One word → Multiple vectors (context-dependent)
Information Source Single-level semantics (averaged meaning) Multi-layer signals (syntax + semantics combined as needed)
Polysemy Handling Cannot distinguish meanings (all senses mixed) Accurately disambiguates (context-aware)

The Hero Behind ELMo: The biLM

Before introducing the biLM, let’s first look at two simple models.

The Forward Language Model (Forward LM): Prediction the Next Word from LEFT to RIGHT.

The idea of a Forward LM is simple. It predicts the next word based on the words that come before it, thereby learning patterns from the left context of a sentence.

Before describing this model mathematically, let’s first understand it through a simple example.

In the sentence “I am eating ____ today”, a forward LM looks at the preceding words “I” “am,” “eating,” and predicts that the next word is more likely to be “rice” or “noodles” rather than “sky” or “book”. In this process, it implicitly learns useful patterns such as the collocation between “eat” and “rice,” and the temporal context shown by “today”.

In mathematical way, we has a word sequence:

t1,t2,…,tN

The forward LM aims to maximize the probability of the entire sequence:

p(t1,t2,…,tN)=∏k=1Np(tk∣t1,…,tk−1).

In practice:

The output inside the blue box is then used to predict the next word (e.g., “rice” or “noodles” in our earlier example), which corresponds to the probability term p(tk∣t1,…,tk−1).

The Backward Language Model (Backward LM): Prediction the Next Word from RIGHT to LEFT.

The backward LM works in the opposite direction from the forward LM.
Using same example “ eats rice”, a backward LM looks at the following words: “eats” and “rice". Then, it infers that the preceding word is more likely to be “I” or “he”, rather than “rock” or “tree”.

In this process, it learns that words like “rice” are commonly preceded by verbs such as “eat”, effectively capturing information that the forward LM alone may miss.

The mathematical formulation is similar, but it is “reversed”.

p(t1,t2,…,tN)=∏k=1Np(tk∣tk+1,…,tN).

In practice:

A forward LM can only see the left part of a sentence, and a backward LM can only see the right part. It’s not hard to see that if we want a model to capture the entire sentence, we can simply combine the two directions → forward LM + backward LM = biLM.

Bidirectional Combination: biLM

Now that a biLM is the combination of a forward LM and a backward LM, our objective becomes maximizing both the forward LM probability (p(t1,t2,…,tN)=∏k=1Np(tk∣t1,…,tk−1)) and the backward LM probability (p(t1,t2,…,tN)=∏k=1Np(tk∣tk+1,…,tN)).

Furthermore, we can rewrite the forward and backward LM probabilities in a parameterized form:

p→(t1,…,tN;Θx,Θ→LSTM,Θs)=∏k=1Np(tk∣t1,…,tk−1;Θx,Θ→LSTM,Θs).p←(t1,…,tN;Θx,Θ←LSTM,Θs)=∏k=1Np(tk∣tk+1,…,tN;Θx,Θ←LSTM,Θs).

Here, Θx represents the parameters of the CharCNN that converts a word into its embedding. Θ←LSTM represents the parameters of the backward LSTM. Θ→LSTM represents the parameters of the forward LSTM. Θs represents the parameters of the softmax layer. All of these parameters are updated during training.

💡 Tip

Q: Isn’t ELMo meant for embedding? Why do we still need a CharCNN?

A: Because ELMO embedding are extracted from the biLM hidden states, not directly from the word itself. You can think of the biLM as an expert whose job is to understand the entire sentence. But before this expert can understand anything, it needs to first recognize letters and words → the CharCNN is essentially the expert’s primer book for learning to read.

Q: Why not use Word2Vec or similar methods, and instead rely on a CharCNN?

A: Word2Vec can not solve the word it have not seen before. And it can not understand the roots and affixes. For example, “run” and “running” are total different vector in Word2Vec. However, since CharCNN[1] is based on CNN, it can use the different size of kernel to get understand the different length of character combinations (character-level) → understanding roots and affixes → better handling of unseen words.

Since we want both the forward and backward LM probability to be high, the training objective becomes maximizing the sum of their log-likelihoods:

log⁡p→(t1,…,tN;Θx,Θ→LSTM,Θs)+log⁡p←(t1,…,tN;Θx,Θ←LSTM,Θs)

Since

log⁡p→(t1,…,tN)=∑k=1Nlog⁡p(tk∣t1,…,tk−1;Θx,Θ→LSTM,Θs)

and

log⁡p←(t1,…,tN)=∑k=1Nlog⁡p(tk∣tk+1,…,tN;Θx,Θ←LSTM,Θs)

ℹ️ Note

log⁡AB=log⁡A+log⁡B

Then,

max[∑k=1N(log⁡p(tk∣t1,…,tk−1;Θx,Θ→LSTM,Θs)+log⁡p(tk∣tk+1,…,tN;Θx,Θ←LSTM,Θs))]

That is the objective function of the following network.

ELMo: Extracting Word Embeddings from Hidden States

First, let’s explain why ELMo uses information from the hidden states rather than the final output Ti (followed by a linear projection or some other transformation).

The top-layer output is biased toward semantic tasks, because the training objective itself is semantic (predicting the next word). As a result, the final representation becomes more abstract and may lose some of the fine-grained information about the word itself. This abstraction is beneficial for semantic-related tasks, since the resulting word embedding already encodes semantic signals. However, for syntax-related tasks, directly using the top-layer output leads to poorer performance.

In other words, different tasks require different kinds of knowledge, and a single final-layer representation can not be optimal for all of them. This is precisely why ELMo leverages information from multiple hidden layers, rather than relying solely on the last output.

Now, let us have a look on the core equation of ELMo:

ELMok=γ∑j=0Lsj⋅hk,j

k represents the position of the word in the sentence. j refers to the layer in the biLM. hk,j is the hidden state in j-th layer of the biLM.

hk,1=[h→k,1LM,h←k,1LM]hk,2=[h→k,2LM,h←k,2LM]hk,0=CharCNN(tk)

The process can also be visualized as shown in the figure below:

After that, h1,j carries lower-level syntactic information, h2,j carries higher-level semantic information, and h0,j captures information about the word itself (roots and affixes).

📘 Important

ELMo does not use the final-layer output Tk. Instead, it relies on the hidden states from the intermediate LSTM layers.

In this way, we fully capture both the breadth of a word (its contextual meaning) and the depth of its information (roots & affixes → syntax → semantic). Then, all we need to do is combine these information for different task.

The way we combine these representations is through the learnable parameters sj and γ. Both of them are trainable weights that are updated with the downstream task.

📘 Important

At this stage, the biLM serves as a pretrained model, and its parameters are no longer updated.

For a POS tagging task, the lower layers are more important, so s1 will receive a larger weight. For a word-sense disambiguation task, the deeper layers matter more, so s2 will become larger. If ELMo provides substantial help, the scaling parameter γ will increase. If the downstream model can already learn strong features on its own, γ will decrease.

From the figure above, we can see that ELMo learns different types of information from different layers and selects the most suitable combination depending on the downstream task. The difference between using ELMo at the input layer versus the output layer is where the ELMo representation is injected into the model.

When used at the input layer, ELMo often replaces traditional word embeddings.

In this case, the model tends to assign a larger weight to s1​, since more syntactic information is needed at the input stage.

When used at the output layer, ELMo is typically inserted into the middle or upper layers of the model. This also highlights one of ELMo’s advantages over traditional embeddings like Word2Vec. ELMo is not just an embedding, but a contextual encoder. It can be placed at virtually any position within a model, not just at the input layer.

At this stage, ELMo functions more like a supplement to the model’s existing representations (similar to a residual connection). Since the model has already learned substantial semantic information on its own, ELMo mainly needs to contribute lower-level features (those closer to the word itself). This is why it places more weight on s1 and s0.

The reason why ELMo is so powerful is quite simple: unlike traditional word vectors which only assign a fixed vector to each word, ELMo utilizes all the layers within the language model. The paper clearly shows that the different layers of biLM have learned different types of language knowledge. The lower layers are more like syntax experts (suitable for POS, structure analysis), while the higher layers are more like semantic experts (suitable for word sense disambiguation, reading comprehension).

ELMo allows the model to decide which layer to use on its own, based on the Task model.

Although BERT became the mainstream later on, the idea of ELMo remains extremely important: The different layers of the language model inherently contain language knowledge of different granularities, and downstream tasks can be combined as needed.
